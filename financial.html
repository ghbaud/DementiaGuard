Financial Protection

Even with device restrictions and scam detection tools in place, a financial safety net matters. These safeguards work at the banking and legal level -- they're the last line of defense if other protections are bypassed.

Things That Often Help

  • Calling the bank to set up fraud alerts and trusted contact designation
  • Considering a spending-limited prepaid card
  • Reviewing legal options like power of attorney
  • Freezing credit reports to prevent new accounts being opened

Call the bank's fraud department. Explain the situation honestly. Banks deal with this regularly and have specific procedures. Ask about:

  • Transaction alerts (text or email for purchases over a threshold you choose)
  • Daily spending limits
  • Blocking specific transaction types (wire transfers, international transactions)
  • Designating a "trusted contact" on the account -- many banks now support this. The trusted contact can be called if the bank suspects exploitation, but cannot access or control the account.

AARP BankSafe Initiative: This program trains bank staff to recognize signs of cognitive decline and potential financial exploitation. Ask your bank if they participate. More info at aarp.org.

Consider a joint account or authorized user status so you can monitor transactions.

Every bank handles these requests differently. If the first person you speak to isn't helpful, ask to speak with their fraud or elder services department.

True Link Visa Prepaid Card: A prepaid debit card designed specifically for situations like this. As the caregiver, you:

  • Load funds onto the card periodically (controlling the maximum exposure)
  • Set spending rules (block specific merchants, categories like "wire transfers," or online purchases)
  • Get real-time alerts when the card is used
  • Can freeze the card instantly from your phone

To the person using it, it looks and works like a normal debit card. They maintain their sense of independence while you maintain oversight.

  • Cost: Check truelinkfinancial.com for current pricing
  • Worth considering if: you want to give them spending independence with guardrails

These are significant legal steps. Many families start with power of attorney and find it sufficient. None of this is urgent enough to rush -- take time to understand the options.

Power of Attorney (POA)

  • What it is: A legal document that authorizes someone to make financial and/or healthcare decisions on behalf of another person
  • Two types: Financial POA (managing money, property, bills) and Healthcare POA (medical decisions)
  • When to pursue: Ideally while your loved one can still participate in the decision and express their wishes. POA requires the person to be legally competent at the time of signing.
  • Important: POA does not remove the person's rights -- they can still make decisions. It gives you the authority to act on their behalf when needed.

Guardianship / Conservatorship

  • More restrictive than POA -- a court formally appoints someone to manage the person's affairs
  • Court-supervised: requires ongoing reporting and court approval for major decisions
  • When it's necessary: when the person is no longer competent to grant POA, or when POA isn't sufficient to prevent exploitation
  • This is a significant step with legal costs. Consult an attorney before pursuing it.

When to involve an elder law attorney: if you're unsure which path is right, if there's family disagreement about care decisions, or if the financial situation is complex.

Laws vary by state. An elder law attorney can advise on your specific situation. The National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys (naela.org) can help you find one near you.

Freeze credit reports at all three bureaus:

  • Equifax: equifax.com/personal/credit-report-services/credit-freeze/ or call 800-349-9960
  • Experian: experian.com/freeze/ or call 888-397-3742
  • TransUnion: transunion.com/credit-freeze or call 888-909-8872

A credit freeze prevents anyone from opening new accounts in their name. It's free, and you can temporarily lift it if a legitimate new account is needed.

Set up credit monitoring: Services like Credit Karma (free) can alert you to new accounts or inquiries.

Check for unauthorized accounts: Review their credit report at annualcreditreport.com (free, one per year from each bureau).

A credit freeze doesn't affect their existing accounts or credit score. It simply stops new accounts from being opened.

Why This Matters

Elder fraud losses exceeded $3.4 billion in 2023, and people who are defrauded in their own home are twice as likely to die or end up in residential care within a year of the incident. Financial exploitation doesn't just drain bank accounts -- it erodes dignity, independence, and trust.

Financial safeguards are the most underused protection available. Many families don't know that banks offer trusted contact designation, or that credit freezes are free and simple. These tools exist specifically for situations like yours.
