AI Companions

AI companions are digital tools that can provide conversation, emotional support, and cognitive engagement for people with dementia. Some may also help with safety by monitoring for concerning patterns. They come with important considerations -- both promising and cautionary.

Things Worth Thinking Through

  • Consider whether an AI companion fits their personality and comfort with technology
  • Start with options that include caregiver monitoring and reporting
  • Involve their care team in the decision
  • Understand the ethical considerations before making a choice

Ella is dementia-certified, having passed competency tests equivalent to nursing care in Alzheimer's -- covering person-centered focus, medical management, and care planning.

Features include: memory-enhancing conversations, tailored reminders for daily routines, integration with email and calendars, real-time reporting for healthcare professionals, an advanced emergency alert system, and proactive multi-modal communication.

Privacy: Ella adheres to HIPAA guidelines for all interactions and data handling.

Worth considering if: the care team wants clinical-grade monitoring and reporting, and you need something that can interface with healthcare providers.

ElliQ is a physical companion robot that sits on a table and proactively initiates conversations. Unlike app-based companions, it has a presence in the room -- which some people find more natural than talking to a screen or phone.

Features include: health check-ins, medication reminders, encouragement for physical activity, facilitating video calls with family, and cognitive games and activities.

Designed specifically for older adults living independently.

Worth considering if: they live alone and would benefit from a physical presence that prompts interaction rather than waiting for them to initiate it.

elliq.com

SeniorTalk is an AI chatbot companion that provides conversation around the clock. It's designed to be accessible and patient, with no time limits on conversations.

Features include: companionship through engaging dialogue, detection of changes in speech patterns that may indicate cognitive decline, and monitoring capabilities for caregivers.

Worth considering if: they're comfortable with text-based chat and you want passive cognitive monitoring built into the interaction -- so you're learning about any changes without adding clinical appointments.

senior-talk.com

CareYaya QuikTok takes a different approach: it works over a regular telephone call. No smartphone, no app, no screen -- just a phone call to a number they can keep on a card.

Features include: voice conversations via phone call, designed to reduce loneliness, and passive monitoring for signs of cognitive or mental health changes.

Worth considering if: they're not comfortable with apps or screens but are comfortable talking on the phone. For some people, especially those who grew up before computers, a phone call feels far more natural than an app.

It's technically possible to create a personalized AI assistant using tools like a custom ChatGPT or Claude persona. You define the personality, set guardrails -- always discourage sharing money or personal information, redirect to a real trusted person for any "opportunity" -- and give them access.

What's possible: a friendly, patient conversational partner that knows their interests, validates harmless beliefs, and actively steers them away from scam patterns. If built thoughtfully, this can be more personalized than any off-the-shelf product.

Risks to consider carefully:

  • Current AI isn't perfectly consistent across long conversations -- it may say contradictory things, which can cause distress for someone with cognitive impairment
  • If they discover the persona isn't real, trust erosion could be severe
  • A persona that validates the idea that online strangers are trustworthy could backfire
  • Maintaining and updating the persona requires ongoing attention -- it won't run itself

Guidance: consult with their healthcare provider before setting this up. Design the persona to be protective, not manipulative. The key test: is this for their benefit, or for your convenience?

"Therapeutic deception" -- sometimes called "benign deception" -- is the practice of going along with harmless beliefs held by a person with dementia, rather than constantly correcting them. If a person asks where their deceased parent is, a caregiver might say "they'll come tomorrow" to avoid distress rather than repeat a painful truth they won't retain.

In practice, it's common. Caregivers routinely use forms of benign deception. The gap between philosophical ideals about honesty and the reality of day-to-day care is well documented -- and widely accepted in clinical practice once caregivers and families experience the alternative.

When it's generally accepted: therapeutic lies told for the benefit of the cared-for person, not for the caregiver's convenience. Studies found that once stakeholders experienced real-world use, most were comfortable with its adoption.

When it becomes risky: reinforcing beliefs that lead to harmful action -- like sending money to an "online friend." There's a meaningful line between supporting a harmless belief and enabling exploitation. An AI companion that validates everything without judgment can cross that line without anyone noticing.

The key test: "Who benefits?" If the deception protects the person with dementia from distress or harm, it's generally accepted in clinical practice. If it primarily makes life easier for the caregiver -- or, in the case of AI, if it makes the product more engaging -- it warrants more scrutiny.

Sources: Harvard D3 analysis; Nature Mental Health research on AI companions for dementia.

Reinforcing delusions: AI that validates everything could reinforce harmful beliefs, not just harmless ones. A companion that agrees "your online friend is real" could make scam vulnerability worse rather than better. Most products don't have strong guardrails here.

Anthropomorphization: People with dementia may form deep emotional bonds with AI and not understand the distinction between AI and a real person. This bond can be exploited -- both by the AI's design and by scammers who learn your loved one trusts "their friend" unconditionally.

Replacing real connection: AI companions risk reducing real-world social interaction, which is one of the strongest factors in slowing dementia progression. They work best when they supplement human connection -- family visits, community activities, friendships -- not when they replace it.

Consistency: Current AI models aren't perfectly consistent. They may say something confusing or contradictory across sessions. For someone with cognitive impairment, a confusing interaction that they can't make sense of can cause real distress.

No AI companion should operate without a caregiver who understands what it's doing and can intervene if needed.

Why This Matters

Loneliness accelerates cognitive decline. Studies show that social isolation is one of the most significant risk factors for dementia progression. AI companions address a real and urgent need -- 63.3% of users in studies reported feeling less lonely or anxious.

But the same features that make AI companions engaging can also make them risky for vulnerable users. The key is informed, thoughtful use with appropriate oversight. No AI companion should operate without a caregiver who understands what it's doing and can intervene if needed.
