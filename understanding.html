Understanding the Problem

Understanding the landscape can help you make better decisions about how to protect your loved one. This page provides context -- why people with dementia are targeted, how modern scams work, and what patterns to watch for.

  • Impaired judgment: Difficulty evaluating whether a person or offer is trustworthy. The critical thinking needed to spot a scam deteriorates with dementia.
  • Preserved trust: Social instincts and the desire to be helpful often remain intact even as analytical abilities decline. Scammers exploit this gap.
  • Impulsivity: Reduced ability to pause before acting. The "wait, this doesn't seem right" brake weakens.
  • Isolation: Fewer people around to intervene or offer a second opinion. Social isolation is both a cause and consequence of scam vulnerability.
  • Inability to recognize patterns: A person with dementia may fall for the same type of scam multiple times because they can't remember the previous incident or connect the pattern.
  • Difficulty with technology: Unfamiliarity with how apps, payments, and online communication work makes it harder to distinguish legitimate interactions from scams.
  • AI voice cloning: Scammers can clone a voice from just a few seconds of audio (often scraped from social media). They call pretending to be a grandchild, child, or friend in trouble. "Grandma, I'm in jail and need bail money" -- in the real person's voice.
  • Deepfake video: Convincing but fake video calls. Still relatively rare but increasingly accessible. A scammer can appear to be someone the victim knows.
  • Romance scams: Fake relationships cultivated over weeks or months. The scammer builds emotional connection, then asks for money. They may use stolen photos of attractive people and claim to be overseas.
  • Social media exploitation: Fake accounts impersonating celebrities or authority figures. Targeted ads for fraudulent products. Private messages from strangers who seem friendly.
  • Urgency tactics: Nearly all scams create artificial time pressure. "Send money now or you'll lose everything." "This offer expires today." "Your account will be closed if you don't act immediately." There is almost never a legitimate reason to send money urgently to someone you've never met in person.
  • Authority impersonation: Scammers pretend to be the IRS, Social Security Administration, Medicare, banks, or law enforcement. They threaten arrest, account closure, or benefit loss. Real government agencies never call demanding immediate payment.
  • Elder fraud losses exceeded $3.4 billion in 2023, according to the FBI. The actual number is likely much higher because many cases go unreported.
  • The global dementia population is projected to nearly double from 55 million to approximately 140 million by 2050. This means the pool of vulnerable people is growing faster than the protections available.
  • People who are defrauded in their own home are twice as likely to die or end up in residential care within a year of the incident. Financial exploitation doesn't just drain accounts -- it destroys independence.
  • AI is making scams more sophisticated, more convincing, and cheaper to execute at scale. What once required a skilled con artist can now be automated.
  • Gift card requests: "Buy iTunes/Google Play/Amazon cards and read me the numbers on the back." No legitimate business or government agency accepts gift cards as payment. This is the single most common scam payment method.
  • Wire transfers to unfamiliar recipients: Wire transfers are nearly impossible to reverse. If someone asks for a wire transfer, it's almost certainly a scam.
  • "Investment opportunities" with guaranteed returns: "Guaranteed" returns don't exist. If it sounds too good to be true, it is.
  • Tech support scams: "Your computer has a virus. Call this number immediately." The popup or call is the scam. Real tech companies don't call you.
  • Government impersonation: "You owe back taxes. Pay now or face arrest." The IRS, Social Security, and Medicare never call demanding immediate payment or threatening arrest.
  • Prize and lottery scams: "You've won! Just pay the processing fee." You can't win a contest you didn't enter, and legitimate prizes never require upfront payment.
  • Charity scams: Fake charities that spring up after natural disasters or holidays. Always verify at charitynavigator.org before donating.
  • Facebook, Instagram, and similar platforms are the primary hunting ground for scammers targeting people with dementia.
  • Fake celebrity accounts: Celebrities don't message random people asking for money or offering relationships. But these fake accounts can be very convincing, using the real person's photos and mimicking their style.
  • Targeted advertising: Algorithms show people what they're most likely to click on. For someone with dementia, this can mean a feed full of fraudulent "investment opportunities" or fake product ads.
  • Messenger and DM exploitation: Private messages from strangers who seem friendly. These conversations can escalate from casual chat to money requests over days or weeks.
  • Why algorithms amplify the problem: Social media platforms optimize for engagement, not safety. Content that triggers emotional responses (excitement, fear, urgency) gets shown more. This is exactly the kind of content scammers create.
  • People with dementia may not distinguish between ads and genuine content, between real accounts and fake ones, or between a friend's post and a scammer's message. The visual similarity is deliberate.

Why This Matters

Understanding these patterns helps you spot early warning signs before a crisis develops. If you know that gift card requests are always scams, you can act the moment you see one on their desk. If you know that fake celebrity accounts are common, you can check their social media contacts. Knowledge turns you from reactive to proactive.
