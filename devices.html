Lock Down Their Devices

Restricting what their phone, tablet, or computer can do is often the single most effective step you can take. Most of these changes take 10-15 minutes and happen in the background -- your loved one may not even notice.

Things That Often Help

  • Restricting app purchases and in-app payments
  • Limiting or removing social media apps
  • Enabling a simplified home screen (Assistive Access on iOS)
  • Setting a Screen Time or parental control passcode only you know
  1. Open Screen Time: Go to Settings > Screen Time. Turn it on if not already enabled.
  2. Set a passcode: Tap "Use Screen Time Passcode." Choose a 4-digit code only you know. Do NOT select the option to allow them to reset it with their Apple ID.
  3. Restrict purchases: Go to Screen Time > iTunes & App Store Purchases. Set "Installing Apps" to Don't Allow. Set "In-App Purchases" to Don't Allow.
  4. Restrict app deletion: In the same menu, set "Deleting Apps" to Don't Allow. This prevents them from removing the protections you've set up.
  5. Limit social media: Go to Screen Time > App Limits > Add Limit. Select Social Networking. Set a daily time limit (even 1 minute effectively blocks the apps after a brief use).
  6. Content restrictions: Go to Screen Time > Content & Privacy Restrictions. Turn it on. Under "Allowed Apps," consider disabling Safari if web browsing is a source of scam exposure.
  7. Consider Assistive Access: This Apple feature (Settings > Accessibility > Assistive Access) dramatically simplifies the home screen, showing only large icons for selected apps. It locks out Settings entirely. This is a significant change -- consider trying it when you can be present to help them adjust.
  8. Family Sharing workaround: If you set up Family Sharing and want Ask to Buy (purchase approval), you can temporarily set their birth year to make the Apple ID appear under 18, enable Ask to Buy, then change the birth year back. The restriction persists.

If any of these steps cause distress, it's okay to skip them and come back later. You know your loved one best.

  1. Google Family Link: Download Google Family Link on your phone. Add their Google account. This gives you remote control over app permissions, screen time, and content filters.
  2. Play Store restrictions: In Family Link, go to Controls > Google Play. Set content restrictions to limit what apps, movies, and content they can access.
  3. Require approval for purchases: In Family Link > Google Play > Purchase approvals, require approval for all purchases.
  4. Disable unknown sources: On their device, go to Settings > Security (or Apps) > Special app access > Install unknown apps. Make sure all entries show "Not allowed."
  5. Lock important settings: Family Link allows you to set a PIN for changing settings, preventing them from undoing restrictions.
  6. App pinning: For a simpler approach, Android's "App Pinning" (Settings > Security > App Pinning) locks the screen to a single app. Useful in specific situations but not a complete solution.
  7. Digital Wellbeing limits: Settings > Digital Wellbeing > Dashboard. Set timers for social media apps similar to iOS Screen Time.

  1. Microsoft Family Safety: Create a family group at family.microsoft.com. Add their Microsoft account as a "Member" (not organizer).
  2. App and game limits: In Family Safety, set screen time limits and block specific apps or games.
  3. Web filtering: Under Content Filters > Web and search, turn on "Only use allowed websites" for maximum protection, or use the filter to block known scam categories.
  4. Standard account: Make sure their Windows account is a Standard user, not an Administrator. This prevents them from installing software or changing security settings. Settings > Accounts > Family & other users.
  5. Disable sideloading: Settings > Apps > Optional features > choose where to get apps. Select "The Microsoft Store only" to prevent installing apps from the web.
  6. Browser restrictions: If they use Edge, Family Safety integrates directly. For Chrome, consider the SeniorShield.AI browser extension (see Scam Detection Tools).

Why This Matters

People with dementia often can't distinguish between a real purchase prompt and a scam. The "Buy Now" button on a website looks the same to them as the "OK" button on a notification. One caregiver reported replacing their father's credit card 12 times before discovering Screen Time controls -- after that, the unwanted purchases stopped completely.

Device restrictions don't solve everything, but they remove the most common path between a scam and your loved one's money. Think of them as a lock on the front door -- not the only protection you need, but a critical first step.
