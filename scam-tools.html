Scam Detection Tools

There are tools that can watch for threats in the background -- scanning messages, emails, and websites for signs of scam activity. They're not perfect, but they add a valuable layer of protection, especially when you can't be there in person.

Things Worth Considering

  • Installing a scam detection app on their phone
  • Adding a browser extension that flags suspicious websites
  • Enabling phone-level call and text blocking
  • Setting up a trusted contact who gets alerts

What it does: Monitors messages, texts, and emails for suspicious patterns. Sends real-time alerts when potential scams are detected. Includes educational quizzes and simulated scam texts to build awareness.

Platforms: iOS app, Chrome browser extension

Cost: Free basic plan (alerts, quizzes, tips). Premium $3.99/month (unlimited scam tests, full resources, priority support).

How to install: Download from the App Store (search "SeniorShield.AI") or add the Chrome extension from the Chrome Web Store.

Strengths: Real-time detection; the fake scam text feature is clever -- it sends simulated scams and uses failures as teaching moments. One caregiver reported their family member with early-onset dementia went from replacing their credit card 12+ times to significantly fewer incidents.

Limitations: Requires consistent use of the monitored channels. Won't catch scams that come through platforms it doesn't monitor.

Why This Matters

Online scams are becoming dramatically more sophisticated. Scammers now use AI to clone the voices of loved ones, create deepfake video calls, and write perfectly convincing emails. The scale is staggering -- elder fraud losses exceeded $3.4 billion in 2023, and AI is making it worse.

These tools can't catch everything, but they reduce the attack surface. Think of them as a smoke detector -- they won't prevent every fire, but they give you a chance to respond before real damage is done.
