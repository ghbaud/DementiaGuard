Emergency Playbook

What to do right now, with calm and clarity.

Choose the situation closest to what you're dealing with. Each scenario includes what to do in the moment and what to do after.

In the Moment

  • Don't argue about whether the person is real. This rarely works and can cause agitation. Instead, try: "Let's wait until tomorrow to make sure everything checks out."
  • Redirect gently. Suggest a meal, a show, an activity. The urgency they feel often fades with distraction.
  • If they're at a computer or phone, you can say "the internet is slow today" or find another low-conflict reason to pause.
  • Remember: most scams create artificial urgency -- "send it today or you'll miss out." There's almost never a real deadline.

After the Moment Passes

  • Check their recent transactions (bank app, credit card statements)
  • Consider adding purchase restrictions to their device (Lock Down Devices)
  • Consider a spending-limited prepaid card (Financial Protection)
  • Block or report the person they were communicating with

The goal isn't to win an argument. It's to create enough pause that the transaction doesn't happen. Each time you redirect successfully is a win, even if the belief doesn't change.

In the Moment

  • Don't blame them -- they are a victim, not a willing participant.
  • Contact the bank immediately -- some transactions can be reversed if caught quickly. Call the number on the back of their card.
  • If gift cards were sent: contact the card issuer (number on the back). Some companies can freeze the remaining balance if it hasn't been redeemed.
  • Document what happened: who they sent money to, how much, when, and through what method.

After the Moment Passes

  • Report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • File a report with local police (creates a paper trail that may be needed later)
  • Add financial protections (Financial Protection)
  • Add device restrictions to prevent recurrence (Lock Down Devices)

Recovery isn't always possible, but reporting helps protect others. Don't let guilt or frustration overshadow the fact that you're taking action now.

In the Moment

  • Stay calm -- knowing about it means you can act.
  • Change passwords on any compromised accounts immediately. Use a password they won't need to remember (you manage it).
  • Call the bank if financial information was shared -- they can flag the account for monitoring.

After the Moment Passes

  • Freeze credit at all three bureaus (Financial Protection -- Credit)
  • Set up credit monitoring for new account alerts
  • Check for unauthorized accounts or charges
  • If their Social Security number was shared, contact the Social Security Administration and consider filing an identity theft report at identitytheft.gov

Acting quickly makes a real difference. Most identity theft can be contained if caught early.

In the Moment

  • Don't confront the belief directly -- "that person is fake" rarely works and can cause deep distress.
  • Listen without validating requests for money or personal information.
  • If they want to send money: use the redirection techniques from Scenario 1 above.
  • Involve their care team -- a doctor or counselor they trust may be more effective than family in these conversations.

After the Moment Passes

  • Block the scammer's account if you can access the device
  • Restrict social media access (Lock Down Devices)
  • Consider whether an AI companion might fill the engagement need they're seeking (AI Companions)
  • Gently introduce doubt over time, not all at once. "I read that a lot of people pretend to be someone they're not online" is less confrontational than "that person is scamming you."

Romance scams exploit real emotional needs. Your loved one isn't foolish -- they're lonely and being manipulated by people who do this professionally.

In the Moment

  • Validate their feelings: "I understand this is frustrating."
  • Keep explanations simple and concrete: "We're making sure nobody can take your money."
  • Don't frame it as taking something away -- frame it as protection.
  • Offer alternatives: "Let's call [family member] instead" or "Let's watch something together."

After the Moment Passes

  • Consider gradual restrictions rather than all-at-once changes
  • Leave some apps or functions unrestricted so they still feel independence
  • Revisit which restrictions are essential vs. nice-to-have
  • Talk with their care team about managing the transition

Some distress is normal and usually temporary. Most people adjust within a few days. If it persists, it's okay to loosen restrictions and try a more gradual approach.
